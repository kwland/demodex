    The study

    Counting mites in Danville

    A small, local study looking at how the number of mites lines up with eyelid signs, symptoms, and who actually ends up getting diagnosed.

    Research question

    How does the number of Demodex folliculorum and D. brevis on the lashes line up with a person's symptoms and collarette level, and which groups of people are most likely to be infested?

    Thesis

    Most people, and plenty of clinicians, do not know much about Demodex, so it often goes undiagnosed and untreated. That gap is exactly why more awareness, routine screening, and affordable treatment would help.

    How the data was gathered

    1

    De-identified records from a Bay Area (Pleasanton) ophthalmology clinic.

    2

    8 lashes examined per patient under magnification with lactophenol cotton-blue staining.

    3

    Mites counted, then graded for collarette level and symptom level.

    4

    43 patients, ages 16 to 90, with the numbers crunched in R.

    What the numbers said

    0

    Average mites per patient

    Across 8 lashes, counts ranged from 0 to 31 (median 8). Even "clean-looking" lids carried mites.

    ↗

    More collarettes → more mites

    The average mite count went up steadily as collarettes got worse (none → mild → moderate → severe), which backs up collarettes as a sign you can actually spot by looking.

    !

    The surprise: symptoms mislead

    The people who said they had no symptoms actually had the highest average mite counts, well above the people with severe symptoms. So feeling fine tells you very little about what is living on your lashes, which is a big part of why this gets missed by patients and doctors alike.

    Why it matters

    What the mites do to the eye

    In small numbers, Demodex is harmless. When they build up along the lid, they can set off a chain of problems on the surface of the eye.

    The tell-tale sign: collarettes

    A collarette is a clear, waxy cuff wrapped around the base of a lash, made of mite waste and skin debris. It is the giveaway sign of Demodex blepharitis. If you see collarettes, the mites are almost certainly there. The catch is that most eye exams never look this closely at the lashes, which is one of the main reasons the condition gets missed.

    What can be done

    Treatment and the access gap

    There are options that work at every price point. The harder part is getting people checked and treated in the first place.

    The real hold-up isn't the mite, it's the system

    Demodex blepharitis is common, cheap to check for, and treatable. What keeps it going is the gap in awareness, routine screening, and fair access to care, and that gap hits older and lower-income patients the hardest. Two fixes would go a long way: make lash checks a normal part of every eye exam, and do more to educate people in the community.

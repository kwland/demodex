    The study

    Counting mites in Danville

    A community-based investigation into how mite load relates to eyelid signs, symptoms, and who actually gets diagnosed.

    Research question

    What is the correlation between the quantity of Demodex folliculorum and D. brevis and the symptoms or collarette levels seen in eyelashes — and which demographic factors are associated with infestation?

    Thesis

    Because Demodex is poorly understood in the community, infestation frequently goes undiagnosed and untreated — pointing to a need for awareness, routine screening, and accessible treatment to reduce avoidable ocular harm.

    How the data was gathered

    1

    De-identified records from a Bay Area (Pleasanton) ophthalmology clinic.

    2

    8 lashes examined per patient under magnification with lactophenol cotton-blue staining.

    3

    Mites counted, then graded for collarette level and symptom level.

    4

    43 patients, ages 16–90, analysed in R for correlation.

    What the numbers said

    0

    Average mites per patient

    Across 8 lashes, counts ranged from 0 to 31 (median 8). Even "clean-looking" lids carried mites.

    ↗

    More collarettes → more mites

    Average mite count climbed steadily from none → mild → moderate → severe collarettes, confirming collarettes as a visible marker of infestation.

    !

    The surprise: symptoms mislead

    Patients reporting no symptoms had the highest average mite counts — well above those with severe symptoms. Comfort is a poor guide to what is actually living on the lash. This is exactly why the infestation slips past both patients and doctors.

    Why it matters

    From mite to malady

    In small numbers Demodex is harmless. In high densities along the lid margin, it drives a cascade of ocular surface disease.

    The tell-tale sign: collarettes

    A collarette is a translucent, waxy cuff wrapped around the base of a lash — a mix of mite waste and skin debris. It is the pathognomonic sign of Demodex blepharitis: if you see collarettes, mites are almost certainly there. Yet most exams never look this closely, which is a core reason the condition is under-diagnosed.

    What can be done

    Treatment & the access gap

    Effective options exist across the price spectrum. The harder problem is getting people screened and treated in the first place.

    The real bottleneck isn't the mite — it's the system

    Demodex blepharitis is widespread, cheap to screen for, and treatable. Its persistence is driven by gaps in awareness, routine screening, and equitable access to care — gaps that fall hardest on older and lower-income patients. Two practical fixes: build lash screening into every eye exam, and expand community education.
